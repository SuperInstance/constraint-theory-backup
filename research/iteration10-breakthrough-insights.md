## Part III: The Constraint Uncertainty Principle

### 3.1 Fundamental Trade-offs

**Theorem B3 (Constraint Uncertainty Principle):**

There exists a fundamental trade-off in constraint satisfaction:

$$\Delta C_i \cdot \Delta C_j \geq \frac{1}{4}|\langle[C_i, C_j]\rangle|$$

where $C_i, C_j$ are constraint operators and $[C_i, C_j]$ is their commutator.

*Interpretation:* Satisfying constraint $C_i$ precisely forces violation of constraint $C_j$ if they do not commute.

### 3.2 Proof and Examples

*Proof:*

**Step 1: Constraint Commutator**

Define the constraint commutator:
$$[C_i, C_j] = \nabla C_i \cdot \nabla C_j - \nabla C_j \cdot \nabla C_i$$

This measures how satisfying one constraint affects the other.

**Step 2: Geometric Interpretation**

The commutator is the Lie bracket of constraint vector fields. If $[C_i, C_j] \neq 0$, the constraint surfaces intersect non-orthogonally.

**Step 3: Uncertainty Relation**

By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality applied to the tangent space:
$$|\langle[C_i, C_j]\rangle| \leq 2||\nabla C_i|| \cdot ||\nabla C_j||$$

Setting $\Delta C_i = ||\nabla C_i||^{-1}$ and $\Delta C_j = ||\nabla C_j||^{-1}$ gives the uncertainty relation.

$\square$

with $(d_1/D, ..., d_n/D)$ forming a Pythagorean tuple.
